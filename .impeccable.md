# AcreLog — Design Context

## Design Context

### Users

Working farmers who are simultaneously the owner, manager, and operator. They use this app in two contexts:

- **In the field / shop**: Dirty hands, bright sunlight, time pressure — quickly logging hours after a long day, reporting a breakdown, checking what maintenance is due before a planting push. Needs to be fast and forgiving.
- **In the office / evening**: Reviewing the full equipment fleet, scheduling ahead, checking maintenance history before a big season. Can be more information-dense here.

Multi-user farms have Workers, Mechanics, and Auditors too — but the working farmer who owns everything is the primary audience. They're not particularly tech-savvy but they're not scared of it either. They trust tools that feel like they were built for their world, not retrofitted from some SaaS template.

### Brand Personality

**Rugged. Reliable. No-nonsense.**

Like a good truck or a quality tool — built tough, no wasted chrome, gets the job done without complaining. Not flashy. Not corporate. Not cute. It should feel like something a farmer would actually trust with expensive iron worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Voice: Direct, confident, practical. Never cutesy, never marketing-speak, never vague.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Material Design 3, pushed hard toward ag-industrial.**

References: John Deere Operations Center (clean, professional, green — the industry standard), Carfax/AutoTrader (practical, data-dense, vehicle-record utility).

- **Primary color**: Keep `#2e7d32` (JD-adjacent forest green) — it's right, it just needs to be used more boldly and intentionally, not sprinkled cautiously
- **Background**: Warm off-white (`#f5f2ee`) instead of cold `#f5f5f5` — a subtle shift toward earthy/grounded
- **Cards**: Pure white (`#ffffff`) on the warm background creates natural separation without extra shadows
- **Secondary accent**: Amber/gold (`#d4870a`) for callouts, warnings, and highlights — agricultural, not corporate blue
- **Typography**: Swap react-native-paper defaults for a more purposeful stack:
  - Headlines/titles: `Barlow Condensed` (bold, confident, slightly industrial) via expo-font
  - Body: `Barlow` regular (same family, readable, no-nonsense)
  - Fallback: system-ui
- **Status colors**: Keep existing semantic system (green=ok, orange=due_soon, red=overdue, purple=broken) — it's correct
- **Spacing**: Slightly more generous than current defaults — field use means bigger touch targets, more breathing room between list items
- **Borders**: Subtle `1px` dividers in `#e8e4df` (warm gray) instead of card shadows everywhere — cleaner, less SaaS-dashboard

Anti-references: No glassmorphism, no gradient cards, no neon accents, no "hero metric" dashboard tiles, no generic blue CTAs, no cutesy illustrations.

### Design Principles

1. **Field-first sizing** — Every interactive element should be comfortably tappable with a work glove on. Minimum 48px touch targets. Status information must be readable in direct sunlight (high contrast, not relying on subtle color alone).

2. **Information earns its place** — Show what farmers need at a glance (maintenance status, hours, category) without making them drill down for basic facts. Dense is fine; cluttered is not. Data-density is a feature, not a problem to solve with whitespace.

3. **The equipment is the hero** — Equipment names, status, and hours should dominate every screen they appear on. The chrome (headers, navigation, filters) should recede. A farmer scanning a list of 40 pieces of equipment needs to find the Deere 8R with the overdue oil change in under 3 seconds.

4. **Reliability signals trust** — Consistent patterns, clear feedback on every action, no ambiguous states. If something is saving, say so. If something failed, explain it in plain language. Farmers don't tolerate software that's flaky with data they care about.

5. **Green means go, not just brand** — Use the green purposefully: primary actions, healthy status, confirmation. Use amber for attention/warning. Use red sparingly for genuine problems. Color should carry meaning, not just decorate.
